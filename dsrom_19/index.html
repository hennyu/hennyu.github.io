Genre categories: classes, types, family resemblance

Genre categories: classes, types, family resemblance

How do we categorize texts?

classes logical, disjunct
types ideal types at the center of fuzzy categories (e.g. prototype theory)
family resemblance overlapping similarities
  • how do we categorize texts?
  • how do we decide, for example, if a text is a novel, a fairytale, or a travelogue?
  • in everyday life it seems easy, but behind our estimations and decisions different ways of categorization can be detected
  • three ways to categorize have been discussed repeatedly in genre theory
  • classes: no overlapping between the groups of texts, each text is only and clearly in one group; stricter than everyday life groupings
  • types: a typological way of categorization implies that there is a certain ideal type of text and that other texts resemble this type to a greater or lesser extent. As a result, the borders of a category are fuzzy, because the same text can be positioned somewhere between two types
  • family resemblance: a more complex way to categorize texts which can be described as overlapping similarities. There can be different similarities between some members of the group, but there are not necessarily the same similarities between all the members.
  • This kind of categorization has especially been used for the analysis of historical genre names (if it is assumed that the same genre name can refer to different similarities between the texts, depending on the historical period we look at)

Family resemblance in genre theory

  • Analogy used by Wittgenstein in his Philosophical Investigations
  • originally used to describe linguistic activities
  • taken up in genre theory since the 1960's (see Fowler 1982, Fishelov 1991, Hempfer 2014)
  • A few more words on the concept of family resemblance which is the main concern of this presentation
  • it goes back to an analogy used by Wittgenstein in his Philosophical Investigations
  • he used it to describe the linguistic activity to use the same word for loosely related concepts
  • this idea has been taken up in genre theory already in the 1960's and has been discussed repeatedly since then
  • transferred to genre theory it means that the same genre label is used for different texts which are only related in part or through intermediate similarities
  • I would like to present two quotes which illustrate the idea behind "family resemblance" a bit more

Family resemblance in genre theory

Consider, for example, the activities that we call "games". I mean board-games, card-games, ball-games, athletic games, and so on. What is common to them all? – Don't say: "They must have something in common, or they would not be called 'games'" – but look and see whether there is anything common to all. – For it you look at them, you won't see something that is common to all, but similarities, affinities, and a whole series of them at that. [...]

And the upshot of these considerations is: we see a complicated network of similarities overlapping and criss-crossing [...]

I can think of no better expression to characterize these similarities than "family resemblances"; for the various resemblances between members of a family – build, features, colour of eyes, gait, temperament, and so on and so forth – overlap and criss-cross in the same way. – And I shall say: 'games' form a family.

Wittgenstein, Philosophical Investigations, § 66-67.

  • applied to genres this could mean: if we have a group of texts called "tragedies" they must not all have one feature in common. Some of them can share the same topics, others the same style or a similar personnel
  • and these similarities can overlap, so that a text is related because of the same topics with another text and because of a similar style with a third text, and so on

Family resemblance in genre theory

In the German-speaking countries, Hans Robert Jauß was one of the first to explicitly reject the unquestioned identification of genres with classes in the logical sense of the word. Instead, he proposed the terminology of [...] 'groups or historical families' [(1972)...]. Shortly before, the term 'family' had also been used by Pichois and Rousseau (1967), justifying the analogy with the argument that 'genres', understood as 'historical concepts' in the same sense as proposed by [...] Jauß, represented a "série infinie d'œuvres particulières, ni absolumment identiques ni totalement différentes" [...]

When examining the texts labelled elegy in various European national literatures from antiquity up to the twentieth century, they, like Wittgenstein's games, reveal no other commonality but their name, elegy [...]

The diachrony of the genre can be represented as a synchronic network of relations, in which each individual text or epochal version of the genre is linked to other historical versions through common features. [...] The genre identiy, then, is not produced by a single trait but by the entirety of all relations among their historical versions.

Hempfer, Some Aspects of a Theory of Genre, 2014: 407, 417, 419.

  • How genre theorists have applied this concept becomes clear when we look at some passages from the article "Some aspects of the theory of genre" by Klaus Hempfer, who exemplifies it with the genre "elegy"
  • the elegy was originally defined only metrically (antiquity)
  • later also by the themes (Roman elegies, erotic, threnodic)
  • we see here that the concept of family resemblance has mainly been applied to the historicity of genres and different chronological phases of a genre

Family resemblance in digital genre stylistics?

  • often: classification (e.g. Hettinger et al. 2016, Schöch et al. 2016)
  • also: clustering (e.g. Calvo Tello et al. 2017), networks
  • family resemblance has not been explicitly modelled yet

Questions and goals

  • How can the concept of family resemblance be formalized & applied in a digital stylistic analysis of genre?

  • Gain new insights about genres (case study):
    • How are the texts within a certain genre interrelated according to different features? Are there subgroups?
    • (How are the texts of different genres interrelated when no strict classification is applied?)

Approach: Network analysis

  • Calculate similarities between texts on the basis of different feature sets (here: Topics, MFW)
  • Mapping of textual similarities onto a network structure

  • Eder (2017): ranking of nearest neighbours = basis for relationships (edges) in the network
  • Here: extension of Eder's approach
    • communities
    • multigraph
  • Eder: discusses reliability issues of visual techniques in stylometry
  • (cluster analysis dendrograms -> combined to consensus tree; but nearest neighbours only)
  • network: importance of runners-up
  • networks good for large datasets
  • multigraph: several levels of connections for different feature sets
  • communities: detection and evaluation (either algorithmically or manually) of groups of nodes in the network

Corpus

  • 250 novels in Spanish language (Argentina, Cuba, Mexico)
  • 118 authors
  • 1830-1910
  • 16,964,984 tokens
  • subgenres: general (142), historical (62), customs (36), crime (6), science_fiction (4)

Methods

  • Feature generation
    • MFW: Python, scikit-learn
    • Topic Modeling: Mallet (McCallum 2002), tmw (Schöch et al., https://github.com/cligs/tmw)
  • Network
    • Python, scikit-learn, NetworkX
    • calculate text similarities: cosine similarity
    • nodes: individual texts
    • edges: connections to nearest neighbours
    • simple graph: for 1 feature set
    • multigraph: for several feature sets
  • Community detection
    • Louvain modularity (Blondel et al. 2008), https://github.com/taynaud/python-louvain
  • MFW: tfidf to normalize the frequency counts, no cut-off, different numbers (from 500 to 3000)
  • TM: 5000 iterations, optimization interval of 100, different numbers of topics (from 50 to 100)
  • Cosine similarity: cosine of the angle between two vectors
  • is not dependent on text length, only: how similar are documents in their composition
  • Louvain method (Doku: https://python-louvain.readthedocs.io/en/latest/)
  • method to extract communities from large networks
  • a greedy optimization method (?)
  • optimizatio of modularity as the algorithm progresses
  • modularity: scale value between -1 and 1
  • measures the density of edges inside communities compared to edges outside communities
  • this value is optimized: best possible grouping of nodes
  • going through everything, so heuristic algorithms are used
  • Louvain: first small communities are found, optimizing modularity locally on all nodes
  • then each comm. is grouped into one node and the process repeated

Decision on parameters

  • 100 Topics
  • 1000 MFW
  • 3 nearest neighbours
  • effect of different parameters on the size of the clusters
  • some tendencies
  • interesting: more topics - less clusters
  • more frequent words - less clusters
  • more neighbours - less clusters

Conclusions

Conclusions

  • network analysis suggests itself for family resemblance
  • subgroups of genres can be identified (and interpreted) for different (& several) feature sets
  • indirect relationships can be found within subgroups
  • digital approach:
    • family resemblance as a more general principle
    • not only for diachronic genre development
    • not only for genre
    • more open than classification
    • communities: delimitation

Conclusions

  • Spanish American historical novel: exists!
    • subgroups not due to country
    • apparently common strategies for topics (which period is treated? love story involved?)

Next steps

  • include other features (places, persons -> named entities; grammatical categories; ...)
  • vary parameters
    • of the features (Topic Modeling settings, MFW)
    • of the network (number of nearest neighbours, community detection algorithm)
  • analyze other subgenres (novel of customs, general novel)
  • look at two or more subgenres together
  • communities: e.g. change size of communities
