1. Sentiments and Genre: Background, Aims, Hypotheses

Sentiments and Genre

  • Sentiment Analysis: computational treatment of sentiment, opinion, emotion in text (Pang and Lee, 2008)
  • Sentiments modelled as polarity values or emotion values
  • Method has been used for genre analysis (Kim et al, 2017; Zehe et al., 2016)
  • Here: analysis of Spanish American novels
  • polarity: positive, negative, neutral
  • emotion: trust, fear, joy, etc.
  • Kim et al.: analysis of prototypical emotion developments in literary genres for English texts
  • Zehe et al.: prediction of happy endings in German novels

Aims & Hypotheses

  • Exploration of relationship between sentiments and genre in 19th c. Spanish-American novels
  • Sentiments as linguistic manifestations of emotions on the textual surface
  • Hypothesis 1: degree and kind of emotionality differs for different subgenres
  • Hypothesis 2: it matters, whether emotions are expressed in direct speech or narrated text
  • Sidegoal: test two sentiment lexica for Spanish
  • second hypothesis here is that not just emotions in general matter,
  • but also whether they are expressed in the direct speech of the characters of the novels or in narrated text

Sentence examples

from El Chacho by Eduardo Gutiérrez, 1884, military novel, narrated text:

Quiroga entretanto permanecía en Buenos Aires, bebiendo en la inspiración infame del tirano las más sangrientas ideas, y recibiendo las más terribles instrucciones.

Quiroga, in the meantime, stayed in Buenos Aires, drinking the most blodthirsty ideas from the infamous inspiration of the tyrant, receiving the most terrible instructions.

from Clemencia by Ignacio Manuel Altamirano, 1869, sentimental novel, direct speech:

— ¡Oh! sí podrá usted, Fernando, sí podrá usted. A una mujer tan hermosa como ésta, lo difícil, lo imposible es no amarla. Es demasiado encantadora para que el corazón de usted pueda permanecer indiferente.

Oh! yes, you can, Fernando, yes, you can. It's difficult, impossible not to love a woman as beautiful as this one. She is too charming for your heart to remain indifferent.
  • assumptions: sentimental novels + love story as basic plot element e.g. in historical or costumbrista novels
  • negative emotions: Cuban anti-slavery novels, Argentina anti-Rosas novels, sociopolitical novels in general
  • anti-slavery novel has been defined in terms of its atmosphere of fear, but also by vigorous interferences of the narrator

2. Data: Spanish American Novels

Data: Spanish American Novels

  • 30 novels, 1840-1910
  • 3 countries:
    Argentina (16)
    Cuba (9)
    Mexico (5)
  • 16 authors
  • 4 subgenres:
    sentimental (9)
    historical (8)
    sociopolitical (7)
    costumbrista (6)

Distribution of novels per decade and subgenre
  • 13 novels before 1880, 17 after 1880
  • subcollection of a larger corpus of 19th century Sp.-Am. novels being prepared in the CLiGS group
  • general literary fiction - not easily distinguishable genre fiction
  • assignment of genre-labels non-trivial task
  • genres as given in titles and subtitles collected
  • assignments made in secondary literature considered
  • many variations fount, interpretive step
  • result: 4 broad types of novels: sentimental, historical, sociopolitical, costumbrista
  • costumbrista: novels of manners in the context of the Costumbrista movement

3. Methods: Sentiment Analysis & Machine Learning

  • for the sentiment features: Sentiment lexica
  • for subgenre classification: Machine Learning

Methods: Sentiment features

Sentiment lexica:

SentiWordNet 3.0 NRC Emotion Lexicon
Miller, 1995; Baccianella et al., 2010 Saif and Turney, 2013
polarity (positive, negative, neutral) polarity + 8 basic emotions (Trust, Fear, Joy, Sadness, Anger, Disgust, Anticipation, Surprise)
117,653 entries 14,182 entries
  • Linguistic annotation
    FreeLing (Padró and Stanislovsky, 2012)
  • Separation of
    direct speech
    & narrated text
  • Sentiment values per sentence
  • SentiWordNet 3.0, an adaptation of WordNet 3.0 for sentiment analysis
  • The two lexica differ in how sentiments are modelled and also in their volume
  • SentiWordNet has polarity values (positivity, negativity, neutrality) for WordNet synsets which range between 0 and 1 and sum up to 1
  • The NRC lexicon, in contrast, has only binary values (0 or 1), but those are provided for positivity and negativity as well as eight basic emotions (Trust, Fear, Joy, Sadness, Anger, Disgust, Anticipation, Surprise)
  • SentiWordNet contains 117,653 entries, the NRC lexicon just 14,182.
  • In order to use the sentiment lexica, the texts had to be lemmatized (for NRC)
  • and annotated with WordNet synsets (for SentiWordNet)
  • Each sentence was annotated with FreeLing and the words with sentiment values were determined using the lexicons
  • sentiment values for the words were summed up for each sentence
  • eight basic emotions of the NRC (Trust, Fear, etc.), a sentence is assigned the emotion with a highest value in the sentence

Methods: Subgenre classification

  • Decision Tree Classifier: easily interpretable
  • sentences aggregated into 5 sections, divided by section length: 150 data points
  • 60 experiments: varying type of features/lexicon, depth of decision tree
  • 5-fold cross-validation
  • The advantage of Decision Trees is that they can be easily interpreted.
  • This is desirable in an explorative analysis interested in the kind of sentiment-based features that are relevant to differentiate novels of different subgenres
  • (even if not the best results in accuracy)
  • Decision Tree: a tree-like model with a hierarchy of decisions. Each node in the tree represents a rule and each leaf represents an answer to the decision problem. In a classification problem, the decision path to each class label can be followed in the tree, in this case, the path to the decision of the subgenre of the novel.

Results for subgenre classification

  • scores for the test set!
  • The depth of the Decision Tree was varied between 1 and 15
  • Restricting the tree depth helps to prevent overfitting and usually leads to a better performance of the classifier on the test set
  • The accuracy is given as the mean F1 score obtained from the cross-validation
  • Four different sets of sentiment features were used
  • Features from the SentiWordNet lexicon (WN) and from the NRC lexicon (NRC), both without differentiating between direct speech and narrated text,
  • as well as WN- and NRC-features with separate sentiment values for direct speech and narrated text (WN speech and NRC speech)
  • The results of all experiments are compared to the “most frequent”-baseline
  • baseline obtained with an SVM classifier, using the 5,000 most frequent words
  • highest mean value being at 0.52
  • almost all of them outperform the “most frequent”-baseline (0.3)
  • the results do not reach the best mean score of the MFW classification (0.57)
  • next step will be to combine both sentiment features and MFW
  • results for the two different sentiment lexica, the NRC lexicon performs better than SentiWordNet
  • although the latter covers almost ten times as many words as the first one
  • highest values for both WN and NRC are reached when the sentiment values are calculated separately for direct speech and narrated text
  • best scores are obtained for the feature set “NRC speech”
  • F1 score: the weighted average of the F1 score of each class
  • F1 score: average of precision and recall
  • precision: correct positive results / all positive results (how many false positives?)
  • recall: correct positive results / all relevant samples (how many false negatives?)

Decision tree

tree depth: 3, feature set: NRC speech

  • based on the best parameters
  • novels with higher values of positive speech are more likely to be sentimental novels
  • sentimental novels are lower values of trust and higher values of anticipation in narrated text
  • path for historical novels includes less positive speech and more fear and anger in narrated text
  • Costumbrista novels are characterized by less sadness in narrated text than sociopolitical novels
  • more trust in narrated text than sentimental novels
  • Sociopolitical novels differ from historical novels in that they have a lower value of fear and anger in narrated text

Conlusions and Next Steps

  • Classification by sentiment features: better understanding of how emotions are expressed linguistically in subgenres
  • Distinction narrated text – direct speech matters
  • For Spanish: NRC Emotion Lexicon better than SentiWordNet, relevance of 8 basic emotions
  • Next: increase corpus size, use other classifiers, combine sentiment and other types of features
  • Classification by sentiment features contributes to a better understanding of how emotions are expressed linguistically in the different types of novels
  • Distinction narrated text – direct speech matters for subgenre classification by sentiment
